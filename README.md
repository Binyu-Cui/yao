## Overview & Project Description

"Yao: Transfigurations" is an interactive installation inspired by the Shan Hai Jing in China, exploring biodiversity and human–nature symbiosis through mythic transformation. 

Participants’ faces and gestures are captured in real time, summoning personalized “Yao"—mythical beasts that emerge and evolve within a dynamic digital ecosystem of rivers, mountains, and swirling lifeforms.

Each movement triggers particle-based responses, generating or dispersing unique creatures that reflect the participant’s presence. As more “Yao” appear and interact within the space, the installation becomes a symbolic ecological environment shaped collectively by human motion.

By allowing participants to inhabit non-human forms and influence the rhythm of a living digital habitat, the work shows the richness of life. It also encourages reflection on the balance among species and the role humans play within the larger ecosystem.

## Supplies & Materials

### Components Used:  
**Hardware**
- Camera for real-time facial and gesture capture<br>
- Projector for visual output onto surface<br>
- Audio interface and speakers for sound-reactive visuals<br>

**Input & Tracking**
- Video Device In TOP – captures real-time camera input<br>
- Face Track DAT – detects and tracks facial features<br>
- Hand Tracking (MediaPipe) – tracks gestures and hand positions<br>
- Select CHOP & Math CHOP – isolate and convert gesture data<br>

**Audio Reactive Elements**
- Audio Analysis CHOP – processes audio input

## Design Justification 

‘ Yao: Transfigurations ' combines mythical symbols with interactive methods based on physical participation. It creates an immersive space where digital technology becomes a tool for telling ecological stories.

The project is inspired by the ancient Chinese text Shan Hai Jing. It uses facial and motion recognition to turn participants into mythical creatures, which represent hybrid and diverse forms of life.

The body acts as an input method in the interaction, following the idea of “embodiment” in virtual spaces. In this view, the body is not just a controller, but a place where people feel, exist, and form identity. In this virtual space, each participant becomes a unique creature, showing the flexible boundaries between species and offering a new view of humans as part of a shared ecological system.

The project expresses current concerns about biodiversity and environmental crisis. It does not show humans as watchers of nature, but as coexisting beings. Through particle-based interaction and generated images, the work lets people to reflect on the connection between humans and nature, and how digital art can help us feel closer to non-human life.
